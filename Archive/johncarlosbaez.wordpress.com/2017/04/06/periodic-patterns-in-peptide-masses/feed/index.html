I weighted the amino acids by abundance and summed their molecular weights (minus H2O) to get a “typical” (natural abundance) amino acid in the peptide. Then I did a similar weighted sum of the number of heavy (non-hydrogen) atoms per amino acid (again weighting by abundance)–this corresponds to the number of vertices in the skeletal formula of the “typical” amino acid. Then I divided the two () to give the “typical” molecular weight of a single vertex in the skeletal formula the peptides. The answer comes out to ~14.2 regardless of what database you use. I used Table 6 here (A = 14.2095) and the “Eukaryota” and “All” rows from Table 2 <a href=”https://academic.oup.com/nar/article/45/D1/D1112/2333931>here (A = 14.1811 and 14.1705, respectively). Interestingly, if you don’t weight by abundance, but simply take averages, you still get A = 14.1531. So that’s my incredibly simple-minded guess: the 14.28 number has to do with the “typical” vertex molecular weight of the dataset used.

]]> By: John Baez https://johncarlosbaez.wordpress.com/2017/04/06/periodic-patterns-in-peptide-masses/#comment-90215 Wed, 26 Apr 2017 15:36:09 +0000 http://johncarlosbaez.wordpress.com/?p=23476#comment-90215 In reply to Greg Egan.

Is Mathematica better at finding roots of high-degree polynomials? (It should be, because they’re paying a lot of people to solve such problems.) Do you have any idea what tricks they use? If you use something like Newton’s method, it’s an interesting problem in dynamical systems. Smale posed the question of which polynomials can be solved by ‘generally convergent iterative systems’ (which are a class of methods more sure-fire than yours), and this paper discusses how they work for quintics:

• Peter Doyle and Curtis McMullen, Solving the quintic by iteration, Acta Mathematica, 163 (1989), 151–180.

They show a polynomial equation is solvable by a ‘tower’ of generally convergent iterative systems iff its Galois group is nearly solvable: it’s built from abelian groups and A5 by iterated extensions. This includes any quintic but not, say, a typical sextic.
