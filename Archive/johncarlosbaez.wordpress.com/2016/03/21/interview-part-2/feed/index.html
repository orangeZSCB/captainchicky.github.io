Interstice wrote:

What would you recommend to someone who knows most undergraduate math (basic abstract algebra, analysis, topology, geometry, etc) but little more?

I think it would be a delightful but lengthy project to understand all of Grothendieck’s main new concepts: abelian categories, schemes, sites, topoi, stacks and motives. (The word “sheaves” feels like it should be on this list, and you need to understand them to understand sites, topoi or stacks—but sheaves weren’t invented by him.)

Unfortunately, I don’t know a book that serves as a gentle tour guide to precisely these concepts. That’s sad: they’re not as hard as they’re made out to be. I have dreams of writing a book called Scary Concepts in Mathematics, which would remedy this problem. But I may never get around to writing that.

So, how about focusing on schemes? They’re fundamental to modern algebraic geometry, and there’s a pretty nice free book on them:

• David Eisenbud and Joe Harris, The Geometry of Schemes.

On the other hand, looking this over, I’m afraid it may contain too much jargon.

So on second thought, I suggest starting with my quick introduction to affine schemes in week 1 of my Fall 2015 category theory lectures. Indeed, this whole course is designed to show how your worldview changes when you start thinking with categories—you might like it, I can’t tell.

General schemes are built by gluing together affine schemes just as manifolds are built by gluing together copies of But if you don’t know what a manifold is, this won’t make you happy!

For most people, it’s probably about 50 times more important to learn what a manifold is than what a scheme is. Depending on what you mean by ‘geometry’, you may already be comfortable with manifolds, in which case schemes should seem like a psychedelic variant on a familiar theme.

The answer depends a lot on how much math you already know, and what kinds.

However, no matter what you know, it’s worth reading this obituary of Grothendieck:

• David Mumford and John Tate, Can one explain schemes to biologists?, 14 December 2014.

These two famous mathematicians wrote an obituary for Nature magazine, which tried to explain in simple terms the essence of one of Grothendieck’s ideas. It was rejected as being too technical. Mumford writes:

The sad thing is that this was rejected as much too technical for their readership. Their editor wrote me that ‘higher degree polynomials’, ‘infinitesimal vectors’ and ‘complex space’ (even complex numbers) were things at least half their readership had never come across. The gap between the world I have lived in and that even of scientists has never seemed larger. I am prepared for lawyers and business people to say they hated math and not to remember any math beyond arithmetic, but this!?

A simplified version was later published in Nature.
