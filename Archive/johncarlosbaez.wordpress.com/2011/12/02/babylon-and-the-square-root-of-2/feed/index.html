Sorry to comment on an ancient post, but I see this interpretation of Robson repeated again and again, and it frustrates me. Plimpton 322 can’t literally be a table of Pythagorean triples, not least because it only contains one of the sides and the diagonal, rather than both sides and the diagonal. But are you claiming that the author of the tablet didn’t think of the numbers it contains as having to do with exact side lengths of right triangles (or of rectangles with their diagonal)? I don’t think Robson’s article supports that. I can’t think of any scholar who says that—even Friberg, who states in his book A Remarkable Collection of Babylonian Mathematical Textsthat the numbers in columns 2 and 3 shouldn’t be thought of as integer side lengths, still thinks the tablet is connected with right triangles.

]]> By: Engaging students: Square roots | Mean Green Math https://johncarlosbaez.wordpress.com/2011/12/02/babylon-and-the-square-root-of-2/#comment-85247 Fri, 25 Nov 2016 12:03:50 +0000 http://johncarlosbaez.wordpress.com/?p=6572#comment-85247 So one of the oldest records of square roots in history would be The Old Babylonian tablet YBC 7289, which dates back anywhere from 2000-1600 BC. It depicts a square with two diagonals drawn and on the diagonals are numbers; when they are calculated, you get a very close approximation of the square root of 2 for the diagonal. Their value for the square root of two was about 1.41421297; I could have my students quickly calculate the square root of two (about 1.41421356) and mention to my students that this is pretty impressive for a civilization without modern day technology. The fact that they used clay tablets for math calculations shows how little they had to work with. Yet Babylon was also one of the most famous ancient cities in Mesopotamia; it’s mentioned multiple times in the bible and they were pretty advanced in mathematics for their area, despite the lack of resources we have today. They used a sexagesimal number system, which is base 60; they could solve algebra problems and work with what we now call Pythagorean triples; they could also solve equations with cubes.
