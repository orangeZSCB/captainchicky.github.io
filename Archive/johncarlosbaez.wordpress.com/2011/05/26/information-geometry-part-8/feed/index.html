]]> By: John Baez https://johncarlosbaez.wordpress.com/2011/05/26/information-geometry-part-8/#comment-26589 Mon, 11 Mar 2013 17:40:25 +0000 http://johncarlosbaez.wordpress.com/?p=3688#comment-26589 Marc Harper sent me an email today saying:

I just uploaded a paper to the ArXiv that may shed some light on the entropy / evolution situation. In the paper, I calculate the entropy rates of many finite population dynamics (Markov processes), including the Moran process and the Wright-Fisher process. The Moran process yields the replicator equation in the large population limit (as the deterministic part Langevin equation of a diffusion-drift stochastic differential equation).

The term entropy rate is a bit of a misnomer — the entropy rate is a natural generalization of Shannon entropy to Markov processes, but not a rate in the dx/dt sense. It does not explicitly involve the population distribution (like the relative entropy in EGT); rather it is in some sense a measure of how much the population distribution varies in the long run of the Markov process. There are several notable results, including:

1) the entropy rate is typically maximized for neutral fitness landscapes, with all other parameters constant
2) the entropy rate goes to zero as the mutation rate goes to zero
3) the entropy rate is bounded below the theoretical maximum for generic Markov processes

(1) and (2) match intuition well (mine at least). The paper is here: http://arxiv.org/abs/1303.1890

The main idea is that “Logic is reducible to evolutionary theory”. He speaks more about commonly accepted systems of logic (deductive, inductive, decision theory and mathematics) as branches of biology, but his theory could apply to nonclassical logic systems.

He sais that logic isn’t absolute but enforced by specific evolutionary processes, that the foundation of logical theories are biological, with no metaphysical status of their own. He starts analyzing behavioral logic of primitive organisms, showing that the algorithm that generates a specific behaviour remembers the results of past evolutionary processes (naming them life-history strategies). Finally, with a system that could simulate different branches of possible behaviours, the reason of complex organisms, including humans, is in fact a bunch of analysis proposition about evolutionary processes.

Unfortunately I can’t comment on The Evolution of Reason: Logic as a Branch of Biology because I haven’t read it. What is his idea/theory?

Still, I can say some stuff:

Clearly some aspects of ‘reason’ or ‘logic’ seem to be present in the behavior of animals, and I’d love to know more about how that evolved into human reasoning… which of course makes heavy use of language and ultimately writing or computation at its most sophisticated levels.

As for ‘classical logic’, it’s probably worth noting that what most people call classical logic is only one of several systems of logic that work quite well. In particular, the idea of binary truth tables and the logical operations ‘and’, ‘or’, ‘not’ and ‘implies’ defined as we customarily do it now came quite late in the history of mathematics; you can’t find them in Aristotelian logic unless you’re willing to take things he said and twist them around quite a bit. I doubt you’ll find them in Buddhist logic either… and the Chinese tradition seems to have been even less concerned with issues of this sort.

So, I don’t think we should take a modern Western mathematician’s idea of ‘classical logic’ and claim it’s some sort of cultural universal that biological evolution was leading up to all along. Ordinary folks use a much rougher form of reasoning, which seems to do well enough to let them have enough kids to pass their genes on most of the time.

Does Cooper talk in detail about how ordinary people actually reason?
