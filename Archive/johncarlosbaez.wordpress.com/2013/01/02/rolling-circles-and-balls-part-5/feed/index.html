Suppose you assign ball S1 a “north pole” at the top. If you had it there on the table you could replace it by a disc, for example a coin. For the convenience of US readers I am actually doing this with two Kennedy half-dollars. So: with coin S1 on the table, showing Kennedy’s head upright, the north pole axis comes out vertically. Now put the other half-dollar (S2) touching the right of the first one, again with Kennedy’s head upright. As per specifications flip S2 about the line of centres, so you get the eagle upright (notionally with the south pole pointing up). Holding S1 stationary, roll S2 about 45 degrees toward you around the edge of S1. Flip S2 about the new centre-line and there is Kennedy upright again!

Thanks, I’ll fix that typo. Agricola’s article was very important to me, since that’s where I learned that the Grassmannians for the split real form of G2 were the 1d null subspaces and 2d null subalgebras of the split octonions. I knew these should be the points and lines of some rolling ball geometry….

The relation between the rolling ball geometry and seems to be indirect.

The 8-dimensional manifold is the space of light rays through the origin in a 10-dimensional spacetime with 1 time and 9 space dimensions. So, it’s a homogeneous space for SO(9,1). Thanks to the 10 dimensions, I expect a lot of connections to string theory, though I only know a few.

The 5-dimensional manifold I’m calling in this article—the space of ways a spinorial ball can touch a projective ball with radius 3 times as big—is the space of light rays through the origin in a 7-dimensional spacetime with 3 time and 4 space dimensions. So, it’s a homogeneous space of SO(4,3)… but also of the split real form of the exceptional group G2.

Probably the best hope of relating them is fitting both of them in some larger context. Both SO(9,1) and the compact form of G2 fit very nicely in a certain real form of E6, since the latter is the group of line-preserving transformations of the octonionic projective plane, So, it seems that SO(9,1) and the split form of G2 should fit in the complex form of E6. This complex form is the group of all line-preserving transformations of the complexified octonionic projective plane, So, I’d look here for answers.

Thanks! I have no such hope myself. For one thing, G2 is fundamentally different than the other four exceptional Lie groups. G2 is the subgroup of Spin(8) that fixes a vector in each of its three 8-dimensional irreducible representations; this winds up meaning that the 5-dimensional Grassmannian for G2—the space of points in the rolling ball geometry I’m talking about here—is also a Grassmannian for Spin(8). That underlies everything that goes on here.

On the other hand, F4, E6, E7 and E8 fit together as a row of the ‘magic square’. They’re the symmetry groups of certain Riemannian manifolds of dimensions 16, 32, 64 and 128, respectively. The first two of these are projective planes in the usual axiomatic sense, while the other two are ‘like’ projective planes in some mysterious sense that needs to be understood. That’s where I’d go to understand these Lie groups better. But the only person I can imagine working on this with is John Huerta, and I don’t think either of us should spend our time this way!
