For the last 5 years my main work on the Azimuth Project has been on “applied category theory”: a project to get category theorists doing more practical things, and to use category theory to develop systems thinking in a more rigorous way. This seems to be working well, so let me show off a bit.

There’s now an annual conference and school on Applied Category Theory! The 2021 school includes two courses that use my work on “structured cospans”. One of them uses structured cospans as part of a software tool that allows one to quickly build models from smaller parts:

• Applied Category Theory 2021 Adjoint School.

Also, my former student Brendan Fong has teamed up with David Spivak to set up a new institute, the Topos Institute, for work along these lines. They have $3.1 million in funding so far, but they’re just getting started.

I am not particularly interested in working on AI and AI risks, since so many smart people are already doing that, and I don’t feel I have any special skills in that area. But it would definitely be good to talk to Tegmark.

After I retire in July, and already starting now, I plan to spend more time explaining math and physics… especially polishing up and packaging the explanations I’ve already written in This Week’s Finds and course notes. My attempt to update the FAQ which you’re seeing here on Azimuth, is part of that. But I actually got pulled into that particular little project because I was trying to understand neutrinos a bit better, to continue my project on octonions and the Standard Model.

REDUCE SEA-LEVEL RISE BY HALF
Due to increased glacier melting and thermal expansion, the Ocean is rising 3-4 mm per year – equivalent to 300 trillion gallons per year. At the same time, rivers pour hundreds of trillions of gallons of fresh water annually into the Ocean.
For example, the watershed feeding James Bay, connected to Hudson Bay, pours 85 trillion gallons of fresh water into the Ocean per year; and the Columbia River in Oregon adds 65 trillion gallons annually.
Even with continuing excess glacier melt, diverting 150 trillion gallons of water per year from these two sources will reduce the rate of sea-level rise by half.
IRRIGATE ARID/SEMI-ARID MIDWEST REGIONS
The water from James Bay can be diverted by canal to irrigate 500,000 sq. kilometers (125 million acre) of arid/semi-arid land stretching from Montana to Texas. The irrigation will approximate 18-24 inches per year of precipitation over the region.
The Columbia River water can likewise be diverted by canal to irrigate the San Joaquin Valley in California (5-10 trillion gallons); and 300,000 sq. kilometers (75 million acres) in the arid part of Northern Nevada and Utah.
FORESTS RECAPTURE ANNUAL U.S. CARBON OUTPUT
The irrigated land in both regions can be used to grow billions of trees. Using half of the combined areas would create 100 million acres of new forests, which would recapture 60-80 tons of carbon per acre, or a total of 6-8 Billion tons per year – more than the total annual carbon output of the U.S.
PROMOTE SMALL FARMS/OPPORTUNITY ZONES
80 million acres (40% of the combined regions) could be set aside to revive small family farming. The remaining 20 million acres could be divided into 3,000 communities (+30 million people) drawing remote employees from tech industries and disadvantaged people who are frightened and sickened by life in decaying cities. The increase in land value of $8-10,000.00 per acre (approx. $2 Trillion) will more than offset the cost of building the canals.
