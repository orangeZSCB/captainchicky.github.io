]]> By: Bruce Smith https://johncarlosbaez.wordpress.com/2020/04/13/bigness-part-1/#comment-161220 Thu, 30 Apr 2020 21:58:52 +0000 http://johncarlosbaez.wordpress.com/?p=27954#comment-161220 I think the “classical” lack of attention to formulating formal systems which permit practical-length formal definitions and proofs has had a very unfortunate real-world consequence: people who want to pursue projects such as CoQ, allowing automatic checking of actual formal proofs as a hopefully-someday-practical tool, have had to overcome an incorrect but strong impression that it’s inherently necessary for interesting formal proofs to be impractically long.

Probably a big reason for that was that automatic computers were not a well-known concept predicted to soon be practical, at the time these formal systems were first devised. Unfortunately, once that changed, it remained a rare realization that formal systems could be adapted to address that problem. Perhaps it still is (in spite of it having been mostly demonstrated to be possible) — I’m not sure.

Description:
Recently, debates about mathematical structuralism have picked up steam again within the philosophy of mathematics, probing ontological and epistemological issues in novel ways. These debates build on discussions of structuralism which began in the 1960s in the work of philosophers such as Paul Benacerraf and Hilary Putnam; going further than these previous thinkers, however, these new debates also recognize that the motivation for structuralist views should be tied to methodological developments within mathematics. In fact, practically all relevant ideas and methods have roots in the structuralist transformation that modern mathematics underwent in the 19th and early 20th centuries.

This edited volume of new essays by top scholars in the philosophy of mathematics explores this previously overlooked ‘pre-history’ of mathematical structuralism. The contributors explore this historical background along two distinct but interconnected dimensions. First, they reconsider the methodological contributions of major figures in the history of mathematics, such as Dedekind, Hilbert, and Bourbaki, who are responsible for the introduction of new number systems, algebras, and geometries that transformed the landscape of mathematics. Second, they reexamine a range of philosophical reflections by mathematically inclined philosophers, like Russell, Cassirer, and Quine, whose work led to profound conclusions about logical, epistemological, and metaphysical aspects of structuralism.

Overall, the essays in this volume show not only that the pre-history of mathematical structuralism is much richer than commonly appreciated, but also that it is crucial to take into account this broader intellectual history for enriching current debates in the philosophy of mathematics. The insights included in this volume will interest scholars and students in the philosophy of mathematics, the philosophy of science, and the history of philosophy.

I did the following: first remove all double negations in B and call this result B’, then remove all double negations in A and call this result A’, then replace every instance of x in A’ with B’. Consequently, when I look at the various sizes of constants and relations, they drastically reduce in size. Of course, other foundations can be used and can replace Bourbaki’s set theory in their books, and this is probably one of the reasons which explains why they didn’t spend too much time on fine-tuning that part. Actually, omitting ordinals (which are only covered in exercises) is in my opinion a much bigger drawback than using some inefficient coding device, or using a global choice primitive (the latter is part, I think, of the same philosophy from which it follows that there is no question about what the real numbers are in Bourbaki…)

But note that any time Bourbaki uses Zorn’s Lemma, they quote the corresponding result from “Ensembles” (where it is actually stated in the form of a fun variant of what is commonly used, which succeeds in “factoring out” a lot of the boilerplate arguments found in many other texts to show that the ordering used is inductive). Except for basic arguments with cardinals, this is more or less the only set theoretic thing that can be needed later for the kind of mathematics that they do (namely, to prove the Stokes formula…).
