Bigness (Part 1)

The French mathematicians who went under the pseudonym Nicolas Bourbaki did a lot of good things—but not so much in the foundations of mathematics. In this paper, Adrian Mathias showed that their formalism was incredibly inefficient:

• Adrian R. D. Mathias, A term of length 4,523,659,424,929, Synthese 133 (2002), 75–86.

He proved that the definition of the number 1 in Bourbaki’s 1954 text on set theory requires 4,523,659,424,929 symbols and also 1,179,618,517,981 links connecting symbols, “without which, of course, the formula would be unreadable.”

One reason is that their definition of the number 1 is complicated in the first place. But worse, they don’t take , “there exists”, as primitive. Instead they define it.

They use a version of Hilbert’s “choice operator”. For any formula they define a quantity that’s a choice of making true if such a choice exists, and just anything otherwise. Then they define to mean holds for this choice. That is, .

This builds the axiom of choice into the definition of . That’s a terrible idea (which goes back to Hilbert).

On top of that, their implementation of this idea leads to huge formulas. First, in they replace each appearance of in with a box, , and draw “links” from each box to the subscript in . This creates an ungainly mess. But more importantly, in the expression , each original appearance of in has been replaced by . Thus, if the variable appears times in and the variable appears times, then the expression contains the variable a total of times.

This creates a combinatorial explosion when we have nested quantifers. And since they define (“for all”) in terms of , both kinds of quantifiers have this explosive effect. Their definition of “1” is rather long, with nested quantifiers, so it becomes huge when you write it out in detail.

And in the 1970 edition of their book, it got much longer! Here they defined ordered pairs in terms of sets rather than taking them as primitive, which is basically a good thing. But this makes their definition of the number 1 much longer. Robert Solovay calculated that this new definition requires

2409875496393137472149767527877436912979508338752092897
≈ 2.4 · 1054

symbols and

871880233733949069946182804910912227472430953034182177
≈ 8.7 · 1053

links. Mathias writes:

At 80 symbols per line, 50 lines per page, 1,000 pages per book, the shorter version would occupy more than a million books, and the longer, 6 · 1047 books.

If each book weighed a kilogram, these books would weigh about 3 · 1017 times the mass of the Sun. Since our galaxy weighs about 1.5 trillion times as much as the Sun, these books would weigh about 200,000 times as much as the Milky Way.

    Well, the goal of Bourbaki is to prove that something can be done, not to do that thing in the most efficient way possible.

    Their definition (as quoted in the first page of the paper that counts the symbols) amounts to: 1 is some set Z (if any exists) such that there exists a surjection to Z from {∅}. It might be simpler to define 1 as {∅} itself, but then this creates junk theorems (MathOverflow question 90820): statements (such as ∅ ∈ 1) that we would like to regard as false if not outright nonsense. The ‹some Z such that› business (which relies on Hilbert's choice operator) means that such junk theorems can never be proven (nor refuted, making them as close to nonsense as possible in an untyped language).

    While I don’t agree with Bourbaki's decisions about what to make fundamental, and particularly their decision to bake the Axiom of Choice into the foundations, I approve of doing something like this. It's similar to how object-oriented programming languages like Java define what can be done with the objects of a program but leave it up to the compiler (or virtual machine) to determine how this is implemented. My answer to the MathOverflow question linked above gives a way of doing this in ZF (and first-order logic) that doesn’t rely on anything that proves the Axiom of Choice (or anything nonconstructive). (Actually, I think that Hilbert's choice operator is an elegant way of handling this, and I blame the axioms for equality for making it nonconstructive. But I digress.)

    Sometimes people say that FOL+ZFC (or whatever) is the assembly language of mathematics, but ordinary mathematics is a high-level language. If you take a program in a modern programming language and compile it into assembly language in a naive fashion (essentially following a short constructive proof that such a compilation can be done), then you will get an unwieldy mess. Since we actually want to run these programs, our compilers optimize the compiled version and cut down this length. With mathematics, we don’t need that part, so turning ordinary mathematics into the language of a bare-bones foundation is always a mess. Bourbaki's handling of bound variables (which is a tricky business for any foundation) causes the combinatorial explosion that makes their definition of 1 so huge, but it's only huge because you compiled it, something that is completely unnecessary for doing mathematics. (And if it were necessary, an efficient compiler could shorten the final result.)

    So while there are a lot of things to criticize Bourbaki for in their Book 1, in my opinion, this isn't one of them.

        Using the unordered pair requires more symbols, but it’s a syntactic redundancy, namely writing instead of just once. There are many other places where similar variations could be made. BUT consider . There’s a residual double negation! If we then run the result through a “remove double negation” simplifier again, then we get the simpler result “y”. This is just a toy model of the generic problem, of course.

        Consequently, when I look at the various sizes of constants and relations, they drastically reduce in size.
