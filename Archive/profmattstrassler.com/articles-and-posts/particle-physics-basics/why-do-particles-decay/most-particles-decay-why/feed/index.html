One of the key ideas that emerge from modern physics is that there is no such thing as ’empty space’. What we call space could be said to consist of the fundamental quantum fields. As Matt explained in this and many other postings, a particle is a regular ripple in its corresponding field, often continually interacting with at least one other field. For example, an electron speeding through ’empty’ space is rippling through its own electron field, and also continually deriving its mass from the Higgs field and its unit of negative charge from the photon field. So we have to deduce that these fields occupy every point of space.
The electron is stable, but the same principle applies to your question about an unstable particle. Suppose it is a muon. Rippling through space in the muon field, it is drawing 207 times as much mass from the Higgs field as its lighter cousin, the electron, while having the same charge interaction with the photon field. After about 2 microseconds, it interacts with the Weak field, which transfers its properties into new ripples in other fields, most likely the electron and neutrino fields.
I hope this is a basically correct and helpful explanation. But, as a non-scientist, I would very much appreciate confirmation and more detail from experts. I am particularly puzzled by how every point of ‘space’ can everywhere contain all the fundamental fields…?
