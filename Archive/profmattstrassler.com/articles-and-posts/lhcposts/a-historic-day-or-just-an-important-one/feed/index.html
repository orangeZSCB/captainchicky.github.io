The Tevatron data isn’t inconsistent with what LHC sees, and it makes a weak case a little stronger, but not by much. And the Tevatron will only collect 1.5 times as much data as it already has before it is shut down in September — so it isn’t going to help much in future. The key to the future is that LHC will collect perhaps four times more data by the end of the year, and a factor of 10 or more by the end of next year (knock on wood or whatever is your favorite material.) That will put the Tevatron out of business except at the very low-mass end, around 115-120 GeV.. So, except for this low-mass region, it is the LHC which is likely to get this all straight, with relatively little help from the Tevatron.
