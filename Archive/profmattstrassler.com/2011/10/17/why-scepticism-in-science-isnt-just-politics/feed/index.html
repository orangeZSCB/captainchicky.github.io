]]> By: Matt Strassler https://profmattstrassler.com/2011/10/17/why-scepticism-in-science-isnt-just-politics/#comment-1389 Wed, 19 Oct 2011 06:42:40 +0000 http://profmattstrassler.com/?p=1361#comment-1389 In reply to Thomas Barton, JD.

First, only a first-rate experimentalist with a tremendous amount of detailed information would be able to “suggest a rigorous experiment [etc.]” that would actually do the job. I can speculate about how one might be done, but I’m not going to embarrass myself in public by spreading ideas that only a theorist could love. If you want something done right, get an expert. Of course the experts *are* thinking about it. And don’t rush them — if you want it done right. A bad design will mean an untrustworthy result — which is the last thing you want.

Second, it is going to be several years no matter what. It takes time to design and build equipment, and then it has to be tested, and then it takes time to accumulate any reasonable number of neutrinos (it took OPERA 3 years of data, and they started building quite a while before that.) So you’ve no choice but to be patient.

For scale: the solar neutrino problem was not resolved satisfactorily for 30 years. The DAMA experiment has been claiming dark-matter detection for, what, a decade? and their signal is still not explained either as an experimental problem or as a real physical effect. I can think of many other examples that took several years to get cleared up. That’s just the way it is.
